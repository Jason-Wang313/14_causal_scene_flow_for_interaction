\begin{abstract}
Scene flow is usually treated as a passive perception target: estimate how every visible
3D point will move. For a robot that must interact, this target can answer the wrong
question. A moving point may be caused by a conveyor, camera motion, another object, or
gravity rather than by the robot action being planned. This paper defines \emph{causal
scene flow}: a dense action-effect field obtained by subtracting the no-action passive
future from the action future. The resulting vector field estimates what motion would
disappear under a do(noop) counterfactual. We prove a simple ranking failure: if a
passive distractor has larger task-aligned total flow than the controllable contact,
planning on total scene flow selects the distractor even though it cannot be caused by
the robot. A runnable synthetic stress test with 80000 trials isolates the
failure. At passive-confound strength 2.0 and passive-estimation noise 0.10, a
total-flow planner succeeds in 0.000 of trials and selects the passive
distractor in 1.000 of trials, while a causal-flow planner succeeds in
1.000 of trials. The claim is intentionally narrow: causal scene flow is
a planning estimand and diagnostic mechanism, not yet a learned real-robot system.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Dense motion fields are attractive for embodied intelligence because they are geometric,
local, and compatible with contact-rich tasks. Scene-flow systems estimate 3D point
motion from stereo, RGB-D, lidar, or point clouds \citep{flownet3d,pointpwc,raft3d,nsfp}.
Manipulation systems then often use predicted motion, correspondences, or affordance
vectors to choose where to act \citep{flowbot3d,transporter,denseobjectnets,se3nets}.
This link is natural, but it hides a causal assumption: the visible motion that is useful
for prediction is also the motion the robot can cause.

That assumption is false in many robot scenes. A drawer may slide because a human opened
it. A cable may move because another object pulled it. A point cloud on a conveyor may
have large task-aligned displacement even if the gripper cannot affect it. A camera may
move while the robot acts. A passive scene-flow estimator can predict these motions well,
yet a planner that treats them as controllable affordances will select the wrong contact
or overestimate progress.

We propose to change the central variable. Instead of planning over total predicted flow,
the robot should plan over the action's dense treatment effect:
\[
  C_a(s,x) =
  \mathbb{E}[\Delta X(x) \mid do(a),s] -
  \mathbb{E}[\Delta X(x) \mid do(a_0),s],
\]
where \(a_0\) is a no-action or passive baseline. \(C_a\) asks which displacement at
point \(x\) would disappear if the robot did not intervene. We call this field causal
scene flow.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it defines causal scene flow as a dense
robot-action effect field rather than a better scene-flow architecture. Second, it proves
a minimal counterexample showing why total-flow planning is not invariant to passive
confounding. Third, it releases a small synthetic stress test and the full literature
audit used to pressure-test the novelty boundary.

\section{Related Work and Novelty Boundary}

\paragraph{3D scene flow.}
Classical and learned scene-flow work estimates dense 3D motion from paired observations,
using interpolation, cost volumes, recurrent updates, rigid-motion embeddings, or neural
priors \citep{sceneflowfields,flownet3d,pointpwc,raft3d,nsfp,flowstep3d}. These papers
make dense 3D motion prediction non-novel. Our claim is not improved correspondence or
lower endpoint error. The difference is the estimand: total displacement \(P+E\) versus
the robot-caused effect \(E\).

\paragraph{Flow and correspondences for manipulation.}
Robotic manipulation systems use dense descriptors, feature transport, object flow, and
articulation flow to guide action \citep{denseobjectnets,transporter,flowbot3d,flowbotpp}.
FlowBot3D is the closest hostile prior because it predicts a dense per-point motion
direction for articulated objects and plans from that field. Causal scene flow is
different only if it separates motion that would happen anyway from motion caused by the
robot. Thus, ``flow for manipulation'' is not the contribution; do-differenced flow is.

\paragraph{Action-conditioned prediction and causal confusion.}
Visual foresight and learned dynamics plan by predicting action-conditioned futures
\citep{visualforesight,interactionnetworks,pushinggrasping}. Causal-inference work warns
that policies can rely on action-correlated but non-causal variables
\citep{causalconfusion,pearl}. Our paper joins these ideas at the dense 3D field level:
the causal variable is not a scalar reward or latent factor, but a pointwise effect field
usable by a manipulation planner.

\section{Causal Scene Flow}

Let \(s_t\) be a robot scene containing a point set \(X_t=\{x_i\}_{i=1}^n\), an action
\(a\), and a no-action baseline \(a_0\). Let \(X_{t+1}^{a}(x_i)\) denote the potential
next position of point \(x_i\) under intervention \(do(a)\). The usual action-conditioned
total scene flow is
\[
  F_a(s_t,x_i)=\mathbb{E}[X_{t+1}^{a}(x_i)-x_i\mid s_t].
\]
The passive flow is \(P(s_t,x_i)=F_{a_0}(s_t,x_i)\). We define:

\begin{definition}[Causal scene flow]
The causal scene flow of action \(a\) at point \(x_i\) is
\[
  C_a(s_t,x_i)=F_a(s_t,x_i)-F_{a_0}(s_t,x_i).
\]
It is the conditional average treatment effect of action \(a\) relative to no action,
expressed as a 3D vector field.
\end{definition}

A simple contact planner can score an action/contact pair by the task-aligned caused
motion:
\[
  J_{\mathrm{cause}}(a,k)=\sum_i w_{ik}\langle C_a(s_t,x_i),g_i\rangle,
\]
where \(g_i\) is a local progress direction and \(w_{ik}\) selects points affected by
candidate contact \(k\). The corresponding total-flow planner replaces \(C_a\) with
\(F_a\). The distinction is small in notation and large in behavior: \(F_a\) rewards
motion whether or not the robot caused it.

\section{A Minimal Failure Result}

\begin{proposition}[Passive-flow distractor]
Consider two candidate contacts, a controllable contact \(c\) and a passive distractor
\(d\). Let \(g\) be the task progress direction, \(P_i\) passive flow, and \(E_i\)
robot-caused effect, so total flow is \(F_i=P_i+E_i\). If
\[
  \langle P_d+E_d,g\rangle > \langle P_c+E_c,g\rangle
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \langle E_c,g\rangle > \langle E_d,g\rangle,
\]
then ranking by total scene flow selects \(d\), while ranking by causal scene flow
selects \(c\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The total-flow planner chooses the candidate with larger \(\langle F_i,g\rangle\), so
the first inequality makes it choose \(d\). The causal-flow planner subtracts passive
flow and ranks \(\langle F_i-P_i,g\rangle=\langle E_i,g\rangle\), so the second
inequality makes it choose \(c\).
\end{proof}

The same statement is stable under bounded estimation error. If the causal score errors
are at most \(\epsilon\) and
\(\langle E_c,g\rangle-\langle E_d,g\rangle>2\epsilon\), the causal ranking is unchanged.
This margin is also the practical weakness of the proposal: estimating the no-action
future must be accurate enough to preserve action-effect margins.

\section{Synthetic Stress Test}

The experiment in \texttt{experiments/causal\_scene\_flow\_sim.py} creates a tabletop
point cloud with candidate contacts. One point has a robot-caused effect aligned with
the task. A different point is a passive distractor whose no-action motion is also
aligned with the task but cannot be caused by the robot. We sweep the distractor's
passive-flow strength and the noise in the passive-flow estimate. Each grid cell uses
2,000 trials, for 80000 total trials.

The result is intentionally stark. With no aligned passive confound, both planners solve
the task. When the passive distractor's task-aligned motion is twice the nominal caused
effect and passive estimation noise is 0.10, total-flow success is 0.000
because it ranks the passive distractor first; causal-flow success is
1.000. This does not show that a learned causal-flow estimator will work
on real robots. It shows that the assumption "large predicted flow implies useful
manipulation affordance" can be false for a reason a total-flow objective cannot repair.

\section{Discussion}

Causal scene flow is best viewed as a planning estimand. It can be estimated in several
ways: paired action/no-action rollouts, a passive world model plus an action-conditioned
world model, interventional robot data with matched scene state, or analytic subtraction
when camera and conveyor motion are known. This paper does not argue for a specific
backbone. A bigger scene-flow network trained on total displacement would still optimize
the wrong variable in the proposition above.

The no-action baseline is both the key and the liability. If the passive future is
misspecified, the residual can erase real effects or hallucinate controllability. Future
robot experiments should measure not only endpoint flow error but also action-effect
ranking error: whether the selected contact moves because of the robot.

\section{Limitations}

The evidence is synthetic. There is no real-robot experiment, no learned RGB-D estimator,
and no claim of state-of-the-art scene-flow accuracy. The literature sweep is broad and
auditable, but its extraction fields are based on metadata and abstracts plus manual
anchor papers, not a complete full-text review of all 1,200 entries. The proposed
definition also assumes that a meaningful no-action counterfactual exists and can be
conditioned on the same state, which is difficult in fast-changing real scenes.

\section{Conclusion}

For interaction, the relevant flow is not simply what moves next. It is what the robot's
action causes to move. Causal scene flow turns this distinction into a dense 3D planning
variable by subtracting the passive no-action future from the action future. The formal
counterexample and synthetic stress test show why the distinction can change the selected
contact. The next step is to learn this estimand from real robot interventions and test
whether it improves manipulation under moving cameras, conveyors, humans, and other
passive dynamics.
