# Literature Map

## Field Box

The field box is 3D perception for robotics, narrowed to dense motion representations that influence manipulation, planning, and physical interaction. The sweep covers scene flow and optical flow, point-cloud motion, robot manipulation, contact and interaction dynamics, object-centric models, causal/counterfactual learning, and action-conditioned world models.

## Twenty-Two Hidden Assumptions That May Be False

1. Scene flow is an exogenous perception target rather than an action-conditioned causal variable.
2. All observed motion is equally useful for manipulation planning.
3. Passive motion can be treated as nuisance noise after training a sufficiently good predictor.
4. The robot-caused component can be learned from passive video statistics.
5. A dense correspondence field is enough to infer what the robot can change.
6. The same flow representation should serve passive prediction and control.
7. Background, camera, and environmental motion are either known or ignorable.
8. Rigid or smooth motion priors survive contact-rich manipulation.
9. Object identity and part identity remain fixed across intervention.
10. Short-horizon displacement is a reliable proxy for task progress.
11. Action labels alone bind observed motion to the robot's actual effect.
12. No-action futures are either static or irrelevant.
13. Planner rollouts can tolerate exogenous motion mixed into controllable motion.
14. Flow evaluation can be action-agnostic without damaging downstream planning.
15. Multi-object causal chains are rare enough to ignore.
16. The sensor sees the contact geometry that created the flow.
17. Passive articulated motion and robot-induced articulation are equivalent affordance evidence.
18. Simulation covers the passive/caused mixture encountered in real scenes.
19. Moving points are better action targets than still points.
20. A stronger backbone can absorb causal confounding without changing the objective.
21. Uncertainty estimates repair causal attribution mistakes.
22. Active data collection alone resolves the distinction between do-caused and merely observed motion.

## Directions Generated By Breaking The Assumptions

- **Causal effect flow for manipulation planning.** Mechanism: Estimate a dense treatment-effect field C_a(x)=E[Delta x | do(a),s]-E[Delta x | do(noop),s] and plan over C_a rather than total scene flow. Why it matters: Changes the central mechanism from correspondence estimation to action-causal attribution; directly breaks the all-observed-motion assumption.
- **No-action world models as robot affordance baselines.** Mechanism: Train a passive dynamics model and treat action affordance as residual controllable progress. Why it matters: Strong but easier to dismiss as background subtraction unless tied to intervention semantics.
- **Contact-gated scene flow.** Mechanism: Predict which flow vectors can be mediated by feasible robot contact geometry. Why it matters: Useful, but can collapse into combining contact prediction with existing flow.
- **Counterfactual scene-flow benchmark.** Mechanism: Evaluate flow models by their ability to predict action differences under matched passive futures. Why it matters: Valuable infrastructure, but benchmark-only is a forbidden weak move without a new mechanism.
- **Multi-object causal-chain flow.** Mechanism: Track direct and indirect robot effects through object-object contacts. Why it matters: Ambitious but harder to support with evidence in this run.
- **Exogenous-motion-aware MPC.** Mechanism: Augment a planner with an explicit passive dynamics term and an action-effect term. Why it matters: A planning formulation follows naturally from the chosen direction, not the standalone novelty.

## Readout

The strongest direction is not a larger scene-flow estimator. It is a change in what the flow variable means for a robot: from total observed displacement to an intervention-defined effect field. This is the one move that survives the hostile set because scene-flow papers own dense correspondence, manipulation papers own action-conditioned behavior, and causal papers own intervention semantics, but their overlap rarely defines a dense 3D treatment-effect field that a planner can directly optimize.
